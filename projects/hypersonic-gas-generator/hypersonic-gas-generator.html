// 01 — background

Background & Motivation

Both NASA and the Department of Defense are pursuing a class of endo-atmospheric hypersonic vehicles designed to sustain stratospheric flight for extended periods. Hypersonic flight — generally Mach 5 and above — presents extreme technical challenges that are generally not encountered in other flight regimes: chemical dissociation, material ablation, shock-induced heating, and coupled aero-thermochemical effects that computational methods alone cannot fully resolve. Experimental testing remains the backbone of advanced hypersonic development.

The problem is access. The existing generation of high-enthalpy test facilities — large shock tunnels, arc-jet systems, and ballistic ranges — is extremely expensive to operate and available to only the most mature, high-TRL programs. Novel concepts and early-stage materials can rarely gain access, creating a bottleneck that slows innovation and increases cost throughout the development cycle.

// The Testing Gap

Arc-jet facilities are the workhorse of high-enthalpy ground testing, but they produce stagnation flows — high enthalpy, near-zero dynamic pressure. Shock tunnels deliver high Mach numbers but with very short run times measured in milliseconds. Supersonic and hypersonic wind tunnels can generate the velocites and pressures but not the temperatures. No facility simultaneously replicates the combined enthalpy and dynamic pressure environment of sustained stratospheric hypersonic flight. And none is affordable for low-TRL research.

This project investigates a fundamentally different approach: repurposing a laboratory-scale hybrid rocket engine as a high-enthalpy gas generator whose exhaust plume impinges directly on a subscale test article — simultaneously simulating both the enthalpy and dynamic pressure of stratospheric hypersonic flight at a fraction of the cost of conventional facilities.

// 02 — concept & approach

Concept & Technical Approach

The gas generator is a hybrid rocket engine reconfigured so its exhaust flow impinges directly onto a downstream test article rather than being used for thrust. A test article can include flush surface pressure sensors, aerodynamic shapes such as airfoils or wedge bodies, or material samples intended for thermal protection system (TPS) evaluation. By positioning the article within the plume, it experiences the combined thermal and mechanical loading of a high-enthalpy flow — conditions that are otherwise only achievable in large, expensive ground test facilities.

The engine can be tuned to simulate a wide range of hypersonic flight conditions by finding an equivalent Mach number based on matching one or more of the following: enthalpy, dynamic pressure, true velocity, stagnation temperature, or stagnation pressure — at a specified flight altitude. Correlations can also be made based on matching heat flux directly to the test article surface. This flexibility allows the same hardware to serve multiple test objectives simply by adjusting engine operating parameters.

Simulated conditions are achieved by selecting the appropriate propellant combination, engine chamber pressure, and oxidizer mass flow rate to generate the target matching condition. The propellant system provides the primary control over plume temperature and enthalpy, while chamber pressure drives the dynamic pressure at the impingement point. Together, these parameters give the gas generator a broad, continuously tunable flight envelope from approximately Mach 5 through Mach 20 equivalent conditions.

// 03 — phase 1

Phase 1 — Initial Validation

The initial feasibility study used the USU Propulsion Research Laboratory's 75-mm hybrid rocket engine burning GOX and ABS — a propellant combination with an extensive testing legacy at the PRL, offering high ignition reliability and well-characterized combustion behavior. The motor was reconfigured as an open-plume gas generator and fired across a 10-test hot-fire campaign, with the first five tests dedicated to plume characterization and the final five conducted with a fully-instrumented test article installed.

The test article was a 12.5° half-angle wedge with a hexagonal boron nitride (HBN) blunted leading edge — an exotic anisotropic ceramic selected for its high-temperature survivability and low ablation rate. To validate that continuum flow existed and that the gas generator could accurately reproduce a simulated flight environment, the wedge was equipped with a Flush Airdata Sensing (FADS) system: ten flush surface pressure ports distributed along the upper and lower surfaces, plus a stagnation pressure tap at the leading edge. By measuring the differential pressure distribution across the test article, the FADS system reconstructs the local angle-of-attack of the impinging flow. Angle-of-attack was varied across 0°, 5°, and 8° between tests, and the FADS-reconstructed values were compared directly against the known geometric setpoints — achieving near-unity correlation at the leading stations, confirming the existence of a continuum supersonic flow field and validating the flight condition mapping from engine parameters to impingement conditions.

// Gas generator exhaust plume impinging on 12.5° wedge test article — bow shock visible at HBN leading edge
// Key Result

Feasibility clearly demonstrated. The gas generator produced impingement temperatures of 1781–1960°C — equivalent to stratospheric flight at Mach 7.85 to 8.25 — alongside dynamic pressures exceeding 35 psia. FADS data confirmed continuum flow and validated the flight condition correlation framework. The HBN leading edge survived over 80 seconds of cumulative high-enthalpy exposure with no surface ablation.

// 04 — phase 2

Phase 2 — Continuing Research

Phase 2 builds on the validated Phase 1 framework and expands the demonstrated flight envelope toward the full Mach 5–20 target range. A key development is the introduction of gaseous nitrogen (GN₂) injected at the nozzle exit plane to dilute and cool the exhaust plume. The GOX/ABS combustion products are energetic enough to simulate Mach 20+ conditions — far above the regime of interest for many hypersonic vehicle applications. By mixing nitrogen into the plume downstream of the nozzle, the impinging enthalpy is reduced in a controlled and predictable manner, enabling simulation of slower, lower-altitude flight conditions closer to Mach 5 and 6. An added benefit is that the nitrogen shifts the exhaust species toward a more atmospheric composition, improving test fidelity for aerothermal studies that depend on accurate gas properties at the test article surface.

// Gas generator with GN₂ injection at nozzle exit — plume cooling for lower-Mach equivalent flight conditions

Alongside the nitrogen cooling work, Phase 2 is expanding the test article library to include candidate thermal protection system (TPS) materials. Samples of ceramics, ablators, and high-temperature coatings are exposed to the gas generator plume under controlled conditions — characterized by their impingement temperature, heat flux, and dynamic pressure loading — to assess survivability, recession rates, and surface degradation. This capability fills a critical gap for early-stage TPS development, where the full flight environment needs to be replicated to understand material behavior, but access to large-scale arc-jet or shock-tunnel facilities is not yet justified.

// 05 — cost & access context

Cost & Access Comparison

The primary value proposition of the hybrid gas generator approach is drastically reduced cost and dramatically improved access — enabling early-TRL hypersonic testing that would otherwise be impossible within the budget and schedule constraints of most university and small-company programs.

// Unique Capability

Conventional arc-jet facilities produce stagnation flows with high enthalpy but near-zero dynamic pressure — they cannot simultaneously reproduce both the thermal and mechanical loading of stratospheric hypersonic flight. The hybrid gas generator achieves dynamic pressures exceeding 35 psia in combination with hypersonic-equivalent enthalpy — a test condition not available in existing arc-jet infrastructure.

// 06 — next steps

Next Steps

A central focus of current work is the development of automated design tools that translate desired simulation outputs directly into required engine parameters. Given a target flight condition — defined by any combination of Mach number, altitude, heat flux, stagnation temperature, or dynamic pressure — the tool will automatically determine the propellant combination, chamber pressure, oxidizer flow rate, and nitrogen injection settings needed to achieve that condition. This removes the need for manual iteration through CEA calculations and bridging correlations, dramatically reducing setup time for new test campaigns and opening the facility to a broader range of users.

Industry partners are beginning to run test campaigns with the PRL, using the gas generator to evaluate proprietary materials and sensor technologies under hypersonic-equivalent conditions ahead of more expensive facility-level testing. This external engagement is validating the gas generator as a practical, repeatable test platform and informing the development of standardized test protocols that can support routine third-party campaigns.

A near-term development goal is the integration of active throttle control into the gas generator test sequence. By dynamically varying the engine operating point throughout a burn — adjusting oxidizer flow rate, chamber pressure, or nitrogen injection in real time — it will be possible to sweep through a simulated flight profile within a single test run. A vehicle climbing through the atmosphere and accelerating to cruise speed experiences a continuously changing enthalpy and dynamic pressure environment; throttle control would allow that entire trajectory to be replicated in a single burn rather than requiring a separate test for each discrete condition.

  1. Complete automated design tool — flight condition input to engine parameter output
  2. Expand GN₂ cooling characterization — map tunable envelope from Mach 5 through 9 equivalent enthalpy
  3. Support industry partner test campaigns with standardized TPS and sensor screening protocols
  4. Integrate digital throttle control for dynamic flight profile simulation within a single burn
  5. Demonstrate compressed-air combustion with O₂/N₂ blends to improve atmospheric fidelity and eliminate GOX dependency
