// 01 — background

Background & Motivation

The rapid growth of small satellite constellations in low-Earth orbit has created a mounting debris problem with no easy solution. Stricter regulations — including the FCC's 2022 mandate requiring deorbit within five years of mission end — have forced the SmallSat industry to develop dedicated propulsive disposal systems. Existing propulsion options are highly capable yet extremely expensive, becoming an inhibitor for more SmallSat-scaled missions to be built and deployed from less established players.

Most CubeSats and many SmallSats still launch without onboard propulsion, relying on natural atmospheric decay that can take decades from typical 600 km deployment altitudes — far exceeding the new five-year regulatory window. The propulsion industry has not kept pace with the rapid miniaturization of satellite bus and avionics technology, leaving a critical gap for affordable, high-performing disposal systems.

// The Propulsion Gap

Hydrazine-based systems dominate in-space propulsion but require costly handling infrastructure, hazmat procedures, and specialized launch approvals. Ionic liquid alternatives label themselves "green" but still require catalyst beds preheated to 350–400°C — a significant power burden for small spacecraft. Cold-gas thrusters are safe but lack the impulse budget needed for reliable deorbit from LEO. Electric propulsion options are also very expensive from a cost and power draw standpoint.

Hybrid rockets offer a compelling path forward: they combine a solid fuel with a storable liquid oxidizer, eliminating the toxicity of hydrazine while delivering thrust-to-weight ratios far exceeding electric propulsion. Nytrox — a two-phase blend of 80% nitrous oxide and 20% gaseous oxygen — adds self-pressurization capability, eliminating the need for a separate pressurization system and substantially reducing system complexity and mass.

// 02 — technical approach

Technical Approach

Ignition

Ignition is achieved via USU's patented low-energy arc-ignition system — the same system developed for the GOX/ABS HPGHP platform. Arc-tracking along the 3D-printed ABS surface pyrolyzes hydrocarbon vapor, which ignites upon oxidizer flow. Startup sequences require fewer than 15 joules, and sequential firings require no additional energy input.

Nytrox Formulation

Historically, the PRL used lower mass fractions of GOX in the Nytrox blend (10-13% GOX), which has had less ignition reliability with the arc-ignition system. This work investigated the pros and cons of increasing the GOX mass fraction - it reduces the density of the liquid phase slightly, but increases the Isp an equivalent amount such that the density-Isp maintains status quo. The benefit that the extra GOX in solution provides is increasing the atomization effect of the N2O liquid phase, reducing the required O/F ratio to hit peak performance, improving efficiency, and making ignition with the arc-ignition sytem 100% reliable and faster latencies.

Injector Development

Four injector configurations were evaluated through cold-flow testing with carbon dioxide before hot-fire commitment: a 30° doublet impinging-jet, an internal swirling 120° full-cone, a reverse-flow, and a staged internal evaporative injector.

// Injector Down-Selection

The reverse-flow injector caused Nytrox to decompose before reaching the rear bulkhead, resulting in catastrophic melting on the first hot fire. The 120° internal swirling full-cone was selected for final testing on the basis of superior atomization, thermal survivability, and consistent regression patterning — despite producing a conical rather than cylindrical regression profile.

Grain Length Optimization

A systematic grain length study varied fuel grain length from 1.0 to 6.0 inches to characterize the relationship between oxidizer dwell time, O/F ratio, and combustion efficiency. The study found that combustion efficiency is dominated by mixture ratio effects rather than kinetic or residence-time limitations — performance optimization should prioritize O/F control over simply maximizing chamber residence time.

Peak combustion efficiency of 95% was achieved at a grain length of 3.5 inches with a measured O/F ratio of 4.4 — near the CEA-predicted optimum for the Nytrox/ABS propellant combination.

// 03 — test campaign

Test Campaign

The development campaign proceeded through preliminary validation, injector cold-flow screening, grain length experimentation, and a five-burn burnout sequence simulating the full end-of-life deorbit mission. All testing was conducted at the USU Propulsion Research Laboratory BLAST Lab.

// 5-burn deorbit simulation test series — single fuel grain burnout sequence

Five-Burn Burnout Sequence

Five consecutive 20-second burns on a single fuel grain simulated the hybrid's end-of-life deorbit burn sequence. Each burn delivered between ~2,100 and ~2,450 N·s vacuum total impulse. The O/F ratio held stable at 2.60 ± 0.06 (95% confidence) across all five burns — even as fuel port area increased by more than 17× and oxidizer mass flux dropped 15-fold. Total propellant mass flow repeatability was confirmed at ±2.54% (oxidizer) and ±5.70% (fuel).

// Key Result

Orbital simulations using measured impulse data from the five-burn test series confirm that a 100 kg satellite starting in a 600 km circular orbit can be fully deorbited from a single fuel grain — perigee drops to 42.3 km after the fifth burn, enabling rapid drag-dominated reentry.

// 05 — conclusions & next steps

Conclusions & Next Steps

The Nytrox/ABS hybrid thruster is a validated, high-performance green propulsion system capable of meeting the SmallSat deorbit requirement from 600 km LEO. Its non-toxic propellant combination, self-pressurizing oxidizer, low-energy restartable ignition, and 3D-printed fuel grain make it uniquely suited to the cost and complexity constraints of small spacecraft missions.

Combustion efficiency improved from below 60% to 95% through systematic injector optimization, grain length characterization, and Nytrox formulation — demonstrating that O/F ratio control is the primary performance lever for this system. These results position the system well for transition toward a flight-ready design.

  1. Environmental qualification — validation testing in vacuum environments
  2. Flight system design — packaging, mass budget, and interface definition for a strap-on deorbit unit
